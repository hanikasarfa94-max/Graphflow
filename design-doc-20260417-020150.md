## Problem Statement

Modern teams already use AI heavily, but they still spend hours in cross-functional reconciliation meetings. Eng, PM, and QA each privately query AI about the same problem, return to their distributed roles, and only then sit together to merge their separate AI outputs into a shared decision. This reconciliation is the unit of waste. AI amplifies individual cognition but does not solve organizational coordination — and that gap is now the binding constraint on delivery velocity.

WorkGraph AI is positioned as the coordination layer that eliminates the reconciliation meeting by making **shared workflow state** the source of truth instead of chat history or private docs. Pipeline is the skeleton, agents are the muscles, humans are the brain.

## Status Quo

What teams in scope currently do today, even badly:

- Eng/PM/QA each query AI individually about the same workflow problem.
- They return to their distributed roles with private partial answers.
- They schedule a meeting (often half a day) to merge those partial answers, surface conflicts, and decide tradeoffs.
- The decision is recorded in a Lark/Feishu doc that goes stale by the next stage.
- The graph state — who depends on whom, what's blocking what — lives in people's heads.

Founder summarizes: roles "go back to their distributed roles, and sat to conference, they meet the demand, the edge. it could be just done by ai."

## Approaches Considered

### Approach A — MergeBot Wedge (minimal viable)
Build only the subagent's 48-hour prototype: 3 textareas, merge button, structured output, emailed decision sheet. Stack: Next.js + Vercel + Postgres + Anthropic API.
- **Effort:** S (human ~1 week / CC ~2 days). **Risk:** Low. **Completeness:** 4/10.
- **Pros:** Validates P-A in week 1 with real user pasted transcripts. Cheapest possible signal. Zero Feishu/IT-approval friction.
- **Cons:** Does not satisfy competition full-scope judging. Doesn't demonstrate "shared workflow state as source of truth."
- **Reuses:** dev.md §10 schemas (Conflict, Decision) as Pydantic models for the structured output call.

### Approach B — Full 13-Phase MVP (selected)
Build PLAN.md as written. All 13 phases. Feishu-native from day 1. Full Python/FastAPI/PostgreSQL backend + Next.js/Tailwind console. Demonstrates the canonical event-registration scenario end-to-end in 7 minutes per dev.md §30.
- **Effort:** XL (human ~6 months / CC ~6-8 weeks of focused work). **Risk:** Medium-High. **Completeness:** 10/10.
- **Pros:** Best possible competition demo. Complete spec realization. Sets up real business product to converge to post-competition.
- **Cons:** 6-8 weeks before any external user signal. High risk of building features the chargeable wedge doesn't need. Sunk-cost exposure if post-competition validation reveals a different wedge (mitigated by Open Questions Q2 below).
- **Reuses:** Greenfield; nothing reusable yet exists.

### Approach C — Parallel Demo + Wedge (creative/lateral, recommended but not selected)
Build two artifacts in parallel: trimmed 6-phase competition demo (intake, parsing, planning-lite, conflict, decision, summary) WITH MergeBot wedge prototype deployed publicly during the build, tested on 5 real teams. Both artifacts share dev.md schemas so post-competition convergence is cheap.
- **Effort:** M (human ~6 weeks / CC ~2-3 weeks). **Risk:** Medium (focus split). **Completeness:** 8/10.
- **Pros:** Wins competition AND validates business wedge in parallel. Operationalizes P-D defer-until-signal. MergeBot user data answers the demand-evidence gap during the build.
- **Cons:** Requires discipline to keep artifacts separate. Demo and prototype could drift if not managed.
- **Why not selected:** Founder selected B because **competition judging requires full-spec compliance** — a trimmed 6-phase demo would lose to teams who built more. Approach C's split focus risks under-delivering on the competition deliverable that is the immediate vehicle for everything else.

## Recommended Approach

**Approach B selected by founder. Recorded rationale: competition judging criteria force full-spec compliance.**

This is a legitimate constraint when the competition is the immediate vehicle. The risk this design accepts: 6-8 weeks of build before external user validation.

**Sequencing (serial, not parallel):**
- **Phase 0 → Competition:** Build Approach B end-to-end. No parallel MergeBot work during this window. Approach C was rejected explicitly because parallel work risked split focus; carrying MergeBot in parallel would re-introduce that risk.
- **Post-competition (Day +1):** Begin Approach A (MergeBot) as a separate week-long project. By then the dev.md schemas are battle-tested, so MergeBot inherits proven Pydantic models.
- **The two artifacts converge** when MergeBot generates user signal that locks the wedge slice (resolves P-D), at which point the chargeable v0 = MergeBot v2 = the slice of Approach B that survives validation.

**Feasibility caveats on the 6-8 week build estimate (per reviewer):**
The "human ~6 months / CC ~6-8 weeks" compression assumes (a) the C: drive blocker is resolved Day 0, (b) Feishu sandbox tenant is provisioned Day 0, (c) one full-time engineer operating CC for 30+ hours/week, (d) no significant agent-eval infrastructure (which P-C technically demands) gets built — only smoke tests. Without a per-phase breakdown and 25% buffer, the 6-8 week number is best-case. A per-phase estimate should be added before kickoff.

Re-engage the same 3 Approvers. Show them the Phase 1-6 build (intake → parsing → planning). Ask: "If this surfaced a real conflict in your last meeting, would it have eliminated the meeting?"
